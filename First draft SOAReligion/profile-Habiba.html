MEET HABIBA ABDELAAL

Habiba Abdelaal came to Ohio University from Egypt to earn her master’s degree in communication and development, and women and gender studies. She is working toward her second master’s in public administration and nonprofit management. Abdelaal took a job at the Women’s Center on the fourth floor of Baker to become a leader for international students and works to represent their community.

ABOUT HER PRACTICE

Abdelaal was born and raised a Muslim in Egypt. The central ideals that outline Muslim faith are called the five pillars of Islam. One pillar, Salat, says Muslims should pray five times a day. This spiritual act gives Muslims time to set aside in their day to connect with their God. Typically Muslims pray at a Mosque, although this is optional for women. Abdelaal frequently prays at home.

THE FIVE PILLARS OF ISLAM

The Five Pillars consist of Shahadah; a recitation of the Muslim profession of faith, Salat; the performance of ritual prayer fives times daily, Zakat; a charity tax to benefit the needy, Sawm; fasting for the month of Ramadan, and Hajj; a pilgrimage to Mecca, if the person can afford it.

  1. Shahadah: sincerely reciting the Muslim profession of faith
  2. Salat: performing ritual prayers in the proper way five times each day
  3. Zakat: paying an alms (or charity) tax to benefit the poor and the needy
  4. Sawm: fasting during the month of Ramadan
  5. Hajj: pilgrimage to Mecca

Source: https://www.metmuseum.org/learn/educators/curriculum-resources/art-of-the-islamic-world/unit-one/the-five-pillars-of-islam

WHERE SHE PRACTICES

Athens has a limited number of spaces dedicated to praying. The two public locations available for Islamic students are the Vernon R. Alden meditation room and the Muslim Student Association at (address).



MAIN LOCATION: HER HOME

Abdelaal has a small room in her house dedicated to prayer. She places her carpet facing toward Mecca and only brings a small candle and her Quran. Since women do not need to attend a mosque, this option is the most convenient for her.

VERNON R. ALDEN MEDITATION ROOM (5TH FLOOR)

The meditation room on the fifth floor of the Alden library is space available for any faith or spiritual practice students and facility need. Since Abdelaal can pray from her home or office, she goes to these public spaces to connect with the Muslim community. Without many locations to go to, the available spaces are small can be far away. Abdelaal doesn’t visit the meditation room often because she can be the only person there, making it more difficult to feel connected to the Islamic community.

MUSLIM STUDENTS ASSOCIATION (MSA) AT OHIO UNIVERSITY

The Muslim Student Association is the closest thing to a mosque in Athens. There is a separated space for men and women and (other accommodations). The space was originally a house before becoming the MSA, and this created some flaws with its design. The space for Women is significantly smaller and Abdelaal believes women are not properly accommodated. “I keep telling (the MSA) that you are making people not want to come to pray because it’s really small, you cannot breathe after a while,” Abdelaal says. She thinks extra space for the children would make more women visit the MSA and help build their community.



LOCAL COMMUNITY

Like Adbdelaal, most Muslims in Athens are international students. Abdelaal says that the group often feels pressure to present themselves to the community, which adds strain of learning to navigate an entirely new country and culture. There is no central group with the resources and connections that are needed to create a sense of community for international students. Abdelaal says the pressure is on Muslims to create their own community, and as outsiders they have a hard time outreaching to new members.

FACING STIGMATIZATION

When Abdelaal first came to Athens, she wore a full hijab that covered her face. After facing racism and fearing for her safety, she changed how she dressed. “I used to wear the veal, but then I decided to adjust the way of hijab to prevent attention,” Abdelaal says. “Wherever I went people starred.” Adjusting to a largely non-Muslim community was challenging to her. She felt she needed to move in groups for her safety and to avoid being a target for racism.
